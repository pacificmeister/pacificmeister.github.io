The Complete History of the eFoil: From 2008 Invention to Global Movement

Most people think the eFoil started with a Kickstarter campaign or a Silicon Valley startup. It didn't. It started with a professor of naval architecture in Stockholm, a carbon fiber surfboard in a university lab, and a question: what if you didn't need waves?

I've been part of this story since 2016 — not as a spectator, but as a builder who open-sourced the designs that helped turn an obscure academic experiment into a worldwide movement. This is the complete history of the electric hydrofoil, from people who were there.

2008: The Invention Nobody Noticed

In 2008, Professor Jakob Kuttenkeuler and researcher Stefan Hallström at KTH Royal Institute of Technology in Stockholm created something the world wasn't ready for: a surfboard that could fly above the water under electric power.

They called it Evolo.

The concept was deceptively simple: mount a hydrofoil under a surfboard, add a brushless electric motor with a propeller, and use a lithium battery pack for power. When the board reached about 6-8 mph, the hydrofoil would generate enough lift to raise the board out of the water. Suddenly you're flying — smooth, silent, no waves needed.

The Evolo project proved that electric foiling was physically possible. But it was a university research project, not a product. There was no manufacturer, no retail channel, no marketing. The world simply didn't know about it.

Jakob's background in naval architecture and composite materials was critical. The hydrofoil needed precise engineering — the angle of attack, the foil profile, the structural strength to handle dynamic loads at speed. This wasn't something you could hack together in a weekend. Or so everyone thought.

2008–2015: The Quiet Years

For seven years, the eFoil concept sat in academic obscurity. A few individuals around the world independently experimented with powered hydrofoils — motorized surfboards, jet-powered foils, towed systems — but no one connected the dots into a coherent, buildable product.

During this period, three things were happening that would matter enormously:

  1. Lithium batteries got dramatically better. Energy density improved, prices dropped, and the RC hobby world was mass-producing affordable, high-discharge packs.
  2. Brushless motors matured. The drone industry drove huge improvements in power-to-weight ratios for electric motors. A motor that cost $500 in 2008 cost $80 by 2015 — and was better.
  3. Hydrofoil surfing emerged. Kai Lenny and other watermen started foiling behind boats and on waves, proving that foilboards could be ridden by normal humans, not just naval engineers.

The ingredients were all there. Someone just needed to put them together and show people how.

2016: The Garage That Changed Everything

That was me.

In 2016, I started building electric hydrofoils in my garage in the San Francisco Bay Area. I wasn't a naval architect or an electrical engineer — I was a maker who saw the pieces and realized they could fit together at a price point that didn't require venture capital.

The first builds were ugly. They were heavy. They were sketchy. But they worked.

And here's the decision that changed the trajectory of the entire eFoil industry: I put everything on YouTube.

Not curated marketing videos. Raw build logs. Here's how I waterproofed the motor. Here's where I screwed up the ESC programming. Here's the prop that cavitated. Here's the one that didn't. Parts lists. Wiring diagrams. Test footage with honest commentary about what worked and what didn't.

The decision to open-source: I could have kept the designs proprietary. I could have started a company, raised money, hired engineers. Instead, I chose to give everything away for free. Not because I'm noble — because I genuinely believed that open innovation would produce better results faster than any single company could. And it did.

2017: FOIL.zone and the Community Explosion

The YouTube videos attracted a global community of builders. People were commenting, asking questions, sharing their own modifications. It was clear we needed a dedicated space.

In 2017, I founded FOIL.zone — a Discourse forum that would become the hub of the DIY eFoil world.

The growth was organic and immediate. Builders from Germany, Australia, Brazil, France, South Africa, and dozens of other countries started posting build logs. Every log pushed the state of the art forward. Someone figured out a better motor mount. Someone else designed a 3D-printable prop. Someone optimized the battery layout for better weight distribution.

Within a year, FOIL.zone had thousands of members and tens of thousands of posts. The collective intelligence of the community was solving problems faster than any R&D department could.

The key innovations from the community

  • Outrunner motors with direct drive — simpler, more reliable than inrunner + gearbox
  • Folding propellers — reduced drag when gliding, improved safety
  • Waterproof ESC enclosures — the eternal challenge, solved through hundreds of iterations
  • Battery management systems — safe, modular designs using widely available cells
  • CNC and 3D-printed foil mounts — eliminated the need for custom aluminum machining

The total cost of a DIY eFoil dropped from $5,000+ to under $2,500. And the builds were getting good — stable, reliable, genuinely rideable by beginners.

2017–2019: The Commercial Awakening

While the DIY community was iterating in the open, the commercial world was paying attention.

Lift Foils in Puerto Rico had been developing their own eFoil since 2014. They launched the Lift eFoil commercially in 2018 — a polished, consumer-ready product priced at $12,000. It was beautiful engineering. It was also twelve thousand dollars.

Fliteboard launched in Australia around the same time, offering a similarly premium product with advanced motor and battery technology.

Both companies were building excellent products. But the price point meant eFoils remained a luxury toy — something you'd try at a resort, not something your average surfer could own.

The DIY community filled the gap. For every one person who bought a $12K Lift, ten people built their own for $2K-$4K. And many of those builders went on to start small businesses, build eFoils for friends, or develop specialized components that improved the entire ecosystem.

A rising tide: The commercial companies and the DIY community weren't really competing. Commercial products brought mainstream awareness and legitimacy. DIY builds pushed boundaries and kept prices honest. Both sides benefited from the other's existence.

2020–2023: Mainstream Recognition

eFoiling went from "what is that?" to "I want to try that" remarkably fast. Several factors converged:

  • Social media virality. eFoiling looks magical on camera. Smooth, silent, above-water flight. Instagram and TikTok did what years of marketing couldn't.
  • Rental businesses. eFoil schools and rental operations popped up in beach towns worldwide, giving people a way to try before buying.
  • Price compression. Chinese manufacturers entered the market with sub-$5,000 eFoils, making ownership accessible to a much broader audience.
  • Celebrity adoption. When Mark Zuckerberg was photographed eFoiling in Hawaii, the sport entered mainstream consciousness overnight.

FOIL.zone grew to 5,300+ members. Over 135,000 posts of accumulated knowledge. The world's largest repository of real-world eFoil building and riding data — all free, all open.

2024–2026: The Next Chapter

The eFoil market has matured. Commercial options range from $3,000 budget boards to $15,000 premium setups. The technology is proven. The sport is established.

But innovation doesn't stop. Three trends are defining the current era:

1. Tow Boogies

Instead of building the motor into the board, a tow boogie is a small, separate motorized device that tows you up to foiling speed on a regular prone foilboard. Once you're up and riding, you release the tow rope and catch waves or ocean swells — a purer riding experience that's closer to traditional surfing.

I've been building free tools and guides for tow boogie builders, carrying the same open-source philosophy forward.

2. Wing Foiling & Para-winging

Wind-powered foiling has exploded. Wing foiling — holding a handheld inflatable wing while riding a foilboard — requires no motor, no battery, no charging. Just wind. Para-winging takes this further with fabric wings that generate more power and require less physical effort.

3. Downwind Foiling

Paddle-powered foiling on open-ocean swells. No motor, no wing, just a paddle and the ability to read the ocean. It's the purest form of foiling — and it's growing fast.

What Matters Now

If you're reading this, you're probably interested in foiling. Maybe you want to build something. Maybe you want to buy something. Maybe you just want to understand the landscape.

Here's what I've learned after a decade in this world:

  1. Open beats closed. The DIY community produced more innovation per dollar than any corporate R&D lab. Not because hobbyists are smarter, but because thousands of builders iterating in parallel will always outpace a single team. The internet made that possible.
  2. The technology is mature. You don't need to be an early adopter anymore. Whether you buy commercial or build DIY, eFoils work reliably. The question isn't "does it work?" — it's "which style of foiling fits my life?"
  3. The community is the product. Tools, guides, and forums are more valuable than any single piece of hardware. Hardware gets outdated. Knowledge compounds.

That's why everything I build is free and open-source. The tow boogie configurator, the build guides, the performance calculator, the AXIS Advisor — all of it. Free forever.

The eFoil started in a Swedish university lab. It grew in a California garage. It lives on in the thousands of builders, riders, and innovators who took an idea and made it their own.

That's the history. The future is whatever you build next.
